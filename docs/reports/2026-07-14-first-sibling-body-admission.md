# One saved Minecraft body entered two honest futures

## Outcome

Behold launched both writable children of the immutable First Life checkpoint
through the production managed-world owner. The Minecraft body `WrenLife`
entered each history from the same saved player data, while two explicitly new
private lives—`WrenDirectCopy` and `WrenAxCopy`—owned different journals,
leases, circle bindings, and Lync files.

This is a branch-local evaluation copy, not a claim that Wren's private life
was duplicated. Neither copy inherited a private turn. Cognition was paused,
no model broker started, and no model decision was made. The proof establishes
the body/life/world boundary needed before a causal mind comparison.

## Reusable boundary earned

`ManagedResidentSpec` now carries two identities:

- `entityId` owns one private continuing life and Lync;
- `bodyUsername` names the Minecraft player body that life may drive.

They default together for normal play. The managed runner rejects two
simultaneous lives claiming one body, but separate isolated world histories may
admit the same saved body under separate evaluation lives. New observations
publish both `self.identity` and `self.body`; historical v2 observations remain
readable. The connection capability is checked against the life and circle,
not accidentally against the Minecraft username.

Each history also receives a minimal isolated vanilla server profile derived
from the parent policy files. It copies no world, log, cache, plugin, or user
cache, and its semantic verifier remains valid when vanilla rewrites property
comments.

## What remains red

This is not yet the direct-versus-Ax causal result. Admission itself advanced
native world time, so these particular children are no longer byte-identical.
The next controlled run must start new children from the immutable checkpoint,
let each mind receive its own bounded native observation, execute one admitted
choice through the normal body, and compare the independently observed
consequences. Exact-request adapter comparison remains a separate control from
checkpoint-matched world-outcome comparison; the report must not conflate them.
